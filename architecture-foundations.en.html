Core principles

Separation

Separation of Concerns

Each part should have one primary type of responsibility. Mixing UI, business logic, and persistence makes change painful.

Cohesion

High Cohesion

Things that belong together should stay close together for easier understanding and change.

Coupling

Low Coupling

Modules should know as little as possible about one another to reduce blast radius during change.

Boundary

Explicit Boundaries

Clear boundaries improve work division, testing, and the longevity of architecture.

Dependency

Dependency Direction

High-level code should not depend on low-level details. The domain should survive framework changes.

Remember Architecture does not appear from nowhere. It is the accumulated result of many small decisions at the class, module, and dependency level.

Architecture Styles

Layered

Layered Architecture

Easy to understand and easy to start with, good for many CRUD systems, but can lead to an anemic domain if loosely organized.

Modular Monolith

Modular Monolith

A strong fit for most new products: one deployment, but with clear modules and boundaries.

Hexagonal

Hexagonal Architecture

Places the domain at the center and adapters on the outside. Excellent for testability and infrastructure replacement.

Microservices

Microservices

Useful when business boundaries are clear, teams are large enough, and independent deployment matters. The cost is distributed complexity.

Event-Driven

Event-Driven Architecture

Great for notification, analytics, and asynchronous workflows, but it requires a strong understanding of eventual consistency and idempotency.

Questions to ask before design

  • What is the business problem?
  • Is the real complexity in workflow, data, or scale?
  • Can the current team operate this level of complexity?
  • Which data requires strong consistency?
  • What can be slow, and what cannot?
  • Do we need faster releases or stronger optimization first?
  • If the system fails, what does the business lose?
  • Which boundaries truly map to business capabilities?

ADR and common mistakes

Short ADR template

  • Context
  • Decision
  • Alternatives considered
  • Consequences

Frequent mistakes

  • Choosing architecture by trend
  • Drawing pretty diagrams with fake boundaries
  • Ignoring data and observability
  • Splitting services too early
