Why this track matters

If `Hard Parts` trains you for difficult distributed architecture problems, then `Fundamentals` gives you the language, models, and evaluation framework needed to talk about software architecture rigorously from the start.

Foundation

Build architectural thinking

Understand what architects actually do, and why architecture is a stream of ongoing decisions.

Styles

See architecture styles clearly

Move beyond names and learn when layered, microkernel, or microservices truly fit a problem.

Practice

Learn technical and soft skills together

Risk, ADRs, diagramming, negotiation, and team effectiveness all get tied back to the architect role.

Main strength of the book This book is especially strong at moving you from memorizing definitions to evaluating architecture as an engineering discipline that can be analyzed, discussed, and justified.

Seven learning modules

Module 1

Architectural Thinking

Understand software architecture, the architect role, and basic trade-off thinking.

Module 2

Modularity and Architecture Characteristics

Study cohesion, coupling, connascence, and how to derive characteristics from domain needs.

Module 3

Measuring, Governance, and Quanta

Use fitness functions, right-sized governance, and architectural quanta to reason about granularity.

Module 4

Component Thinking

Identify components, map requirements to them, and assess granularity at the system-organization level.

Module 5

Architecture Styles Survey

Compare layered, pipeline, microkernel, service-based, event-driven, SOA, microservices, and space-based architectures.

Module 6

Decisions, Risk, and Diagramming

Write ADRs, analyze risk, and use diagrams as communication tools instead of decoration.

Module 7

Team and Leadership

Improve communication, facilitation, negotiation, and the technical leadership side of architecture.

Six-week schedule

Suggested pace

  • Week 1: chapters 1-3, summarize the architect role, modularity, and coupling
  • Week 2: chapters 4-6, identify characteristics and fitness functions
  • Week 3: chapters 7-8, draw quanta and a component map
  • Week 4: chapters 9-14, compare layered, microkernel, service-based, and event-driven styles
  • Week 5: chapters 15-18, compare microservices, SOA, space-based, and style selection
  • Week 6: chapters 19-24, write ADRs, run a risk review, draw diagrams, and reflect on leadership
The best way to study it With this book, the best loop is: read a chapter, extract an evaluation model, apply it to a real case, and rewrite the outcome as a decision or comparison table.

How it fits into the current course

Recommended order

  • Use `Roadmap` for the full picture
  • Study `Fundamentals` to build software architecture foundations
  • Use `Architecture Foundations` to reinforce boundaries, dependencies, and NFRs
  • Use `System Design Exercises` to practice style selection, diagramming, and risk analysis
  • Then move to `Hard Parts` for deeper distributed trade-off work

How it differs from Hard Parts

  • `Fundamentals`: foundations, style survey, architect role, governance
  • `Hard Parts`: data ownership, workflows, sagas, distributed complexity
  • The two tracks complement each other well when learned in that order

Signals that the track clicked

  • You describe architecture through characteristics, not only through component diagrams.
  • You can distinguish architecture style, design pattern, component, and deployment concern.
  • You choose styles based on NFRs, team capability, and trade-offs.
  • You write ADRs, draw diagrams, and talk about risk more clearly than before.

Biggest value of the track

  • It gives you a shared language for architecture discussions.
  • It helps you avoid choosing styles by trend.
  • It lays the groundwork for deeper distributed architecture topics later.
